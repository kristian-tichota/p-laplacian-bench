\subsection{Regularization Sensitivity}

Figure \ref{fig:epsilon-sweep} plots solver execution times as the regularization parameter \(\varepsilon\) is reduced from \(10^{-2}\) to \(10^{-10}\). Decreasing \(\varepsilon\) drives the \(p\)-Laplacian closer to its singular limit, increasing the stiffness of the nonlinear problem. The grid resolution is fixed at \(N_x = 1000\) with a solver tolerance of \(10^{-6}\). The other parameters are fixed at \(T=0.05\) and \(p = 2.5\).

LSODA handles this escalating difficulty with minimal performance degradation. Its execution time remains relatively stable, increasing from 0.076 seconds at \(\varepsilon = 10^{-2}\) to just 0.124 seconds at \(\varepsilon = 10^{-10}\). BDF and Radau are noticeably more sensitive to the tightening physics. Their execution times more than double as \(\varepsilon\) drops, accompanied by substantial increases in internal function and Jacobian evaluations. 

Interestingly, all three solvers appear to reach a computational plateau beyond \(\varepsilon = 10^{-6}\). Continuing to shrink the parameter down to \(10^{-10}\) results in negligible changes; execution times and evaluation counts remain largely flat. This suggests the impact of the regularization parameter on the system's numerical stiffness plateaus around the \(10^{-6}\) threshold for this spatial resolution. Beyond this point, further reductions in \(\varepsilon\) do not seem to induce additional computational overhead. Across the entire sweep, LSODA consistently demonstrates the lowest execution times and greatest relative stability.
