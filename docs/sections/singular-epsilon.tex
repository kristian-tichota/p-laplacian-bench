\subsection{Singular Epsilon}
Figure \ref{fig:singular-epsilon} details the breaking points of the LSODA and CVODE solvers as \(p \to 1\) and \(\varepsilon \to 0\). The solvers operate under very strict tolerances \(\texttt{rtol} = \texttt{atol} = 10^{-13}\) with \(N_x=1000\).

As the nonlinearity index \(p\) approaches 1, the diffusion operator becomes highly singular in regions where the spatial gradient is near zero. The regularization parameter \(\varepsilon\) masks this singularity; however, driving \(\varepsilon \to 0\) exposes the numerical integrators to extreme stiffness and severely ill-conditioned Jacobians. The heatmap illustrates that neither solver is universally resilient.

LSODA demonstrates a predictable failure boundary. At highly singular indices (\(p \leq 1.05\)), it consistently encounters catastrophic failures (indicated in red) once \(\varepsilon\) drops below \(10^{-8}\), exiting with the error \lstinline|scipy/integrate/_ivp/lsoda.py:161: UserWarning: lsoda: Repeated convergence failures (perhaps bad Jacobian or tolerances)|.

Yet, at a slightly elevated index of \(p = 1.25\), LSODA manages to integrate the system across the entire regularization sweep down to \(\varepsilon = 10^{-30}\). Nonetheless, this survival comes at a steep computational cost, visibly indicated by the transition into green and yellow execution times as the stiffness intensifies.

CVODE, on the other hand, exhibits a rather erratic failure topology. It generally collapses at \(\varepsilon \leq 10^{-12}\) for indices like \(p = 1.25\) and \(p = 1.01\) with the error \lstinline|sundials/src/cvode/cvode.c:1487 [CVode] At t = 4.38156039034776e-09, mxstep steps taken before reaching tout|.

However, there is an anomalous stability pocket at \(p=1.05\). In this regime, CVODE successfully evaluates the physics down to \(\varepsilon=10^{-14}\) without significant time penalties, before finally encountering a catastrophic failure at the \(10^{-30}\) extreme.

Interestingly, the catastrophic failures observed in the CVODE backend within this singular regime are highly sensitive to the distance of the first evaluation target (\texttt{tout}). Because the spatial interior is initialized to zero, the vanishing initial gradient coupled with a small \(\varepsilon\) evaluates to a large diffusion coefficient at \(t = 0\). When the solver is assigned a distant output evaluation target, e.g., \(T = 0.05\), its internal heuristics likely attempt to maximize computational efficiency by estimating a proportionally large initial time step \(h_0\). This might push the internal Newton iterations into a regime of non-convergence and consequently trap the solver in a step-size reduction loop until it exhausts its maximum internal step limit (exiting with a SUNDIALS flag -1 error). When providing a proximate initial \(\texttt{tout}\) and evenly spaced out checkpoints to \(T=0.05\), the CVODE solver finishes successfully.

Figure \ref{fig:singular-epsilon-radau} presents the execution time matrix for the Radau solver under substantially harsher conditions. When evaluated at a reduced spatial resolution of \(N_x = 100\) for reasonable computation times, Radau proves extremely robust and stable. Unlike CVODE and LSODA, which would crash even at this reduced spatial resolution, it successfully completes the parameter sweep without convergence failures. However, its remarkably rapid execution times (e.g., \(< 0.5\) seconds for \(\varepsilon \le 10^{-30}\)) are likely a byproduct of its strict L-stability. Upon encountering the stiff initial diffusion, the solver probably instantly damps the infinite transient and thus bypasses the physical resolution of the intermediate dynamics.
