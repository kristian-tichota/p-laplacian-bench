\subsection{FDM vs.\ FEM Discretization}

Figure \ref{fig:discretization-comparison} evaluates the performance of the finite-difference (FDM) and finite-element (FEM, FEniCSx) discretizations when both configurations utilize analytically derived Jacobians. Operating at a fixed spatial resolution of \(N_x = 5000\), a strict tolerance of \(10^{-10}\), and a regularization parameter of \(\varepsilon = 10^{-10}\), the test sweeps the nonlinearity index \(p\) from 3 to 6 across the LSODA and CVODE solvers. The results indicate that FDM provides consistently faster execution times across all tested regimes. Specifically, at \(p = 4\), LSODA requires 24.4 seconds under FDM compared to 37.4 seconds under FEM, representing a 53\% computational overhead. CVODE displays a parallel trend, completing the integration in 14.0 seconds with FDM versus 21.7 seconds with FEM. This performance gap uniformly persists across the entire parameter sweep, with FEM execution times remaining approximately 1.5 to 2 times higher than their FDM counterparts.

This computational overhead primarily stems from the matrix assembly operations inherent to the weak variational formulation. By contrast, FDM entirely circumvents this assembly phase by evaluating algebraic stencil expressions directly on the structured spatial grid. However, the FEM approach retains significant practical advantages. Notably, the exact analytical Jacobian is automatically generated from the Unified Form Language (UFL) definition of the residual, eliminating the need for complex manual derivations. Furthermore, the FEniCSx infrastructure seamlessly scales to higher spatial dimensions without requiring modifications to the underlying solver architecture, whereas finite-difference stencils necessitate complete manual re-derivation. While FDM provides superior computational speed for one-dimensional benchmarks, the geometrical flexibility and automated derivative capabilities of FEM make it highly advantageous for multi-dimensional simulations.
