\subsection{Nonlinearity Index Scaling}

Figure \ref{fig:p-sweep} illustrates the computational cost across distinct physical regimes by sweeping the power index \(p\) from 1.5 to 4.0. The grid resolution is fixed at \(N_x = 1000\) with a solver tolerance of \(10^{-6}\) and a regularization parameter of \(\varepsilon = 10^{-4}\). The simulation time is fixed at \(T = 0.05\).

All three solvers reach their minimum execution time at the \(p=2.0\) mark. This behavior is mathematically expected: at \(p=2\), the nonlinearity vanishes entirely, reducing the system to the standard, linear heat equation. The system Jacobian becomes constant, requiring minimal updates. For instance, the BDF solver completes the \(p=2.0\) case in just 0.038 seconds, needing only a single Jacobian evaluation across the entire simulation time.

Moving away from the linear baseline exposes substantial differences in solver efficiency. In the singular regime (\(p < 2\)), execution times rise moderately as the diffusion coefficient attempts to diverge in regions with flat gradients. However, a more demanding stress test occurs in the degenerate regime (\(p > 2\)). Here, the diffusion term approaches zero where the gradient is small, generating sharp, slow-moving spatial fronts that force solvers to take microscopic time steps. 

BDF and Radau experience a substantial increase in execution time as \(p\) scales up. At \(p=4.0\), BDF requires 9.11 seconds to resolve the physics, while Radau takes 11.84 seconds, performing 38,201 function evaluations and 2,122 Jacobian updates to navigate the stiffness. In contrast, LSODA handles the degenerate regime highly efficiently. Even at the \(p=4.0\) limit, LSODA completes the integration in 0.53 seconds. Its internal heuristics appear better suited to manage the sharp, shifting gradients than the pure implicit methods in this setup. This widens the performance gap by over an order of magnitude at the extremes of the tested parameter space.
